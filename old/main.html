Adrian de Wynter grew up in a small coastal town in Maine, where early encounters with tide pool ecosystems sparked a lifelong fascination with complex systems. She pursued her undergraduate studies at Stanford University, where she double-majored in Physics and Computer Science, graduating summa cum laude in 2005. It was during an undergraduate research project on neural signal processing that she first glimpsed the profound computational principles underlying biological brains.

She earned her PhD in Computational Neuroscience from University College London in 2010, working with Prof. Michael Hartley on recurrent network models of visual cortex. Her dissertation, "Temporal Dynamics of Predictive Coding in Hierarchical Neural Circuits," received the British Neuroscience Association's Outstanding Thesis Award and laid the groundwork for her current research program.

Following a postdoctoral fellowship at the Gatsby Computational Neuroscience Unit (2010–2013), Jane joined MIT as an Assistant Professor in 2013 and was promoted to Associate Professor with tenure in 2019. She founded the Neural Computation Lab, which currently comprises 4 postdocs, 8 PhD students, and 3 research engineers. The lab is known for its interdisciplinary approach, combining large-scale neural recordings in behaving animals with principled machine learning theory.

Beyond research, Jane is passionate about science communication and mentorship. She has mentored over 30 undergraduate researchers, many of whom have gone on to top PhD programs. She co-founded the Brains & Bytes public lecture series at MIT and serves on the editorial boards of Neuron, PLOS Computational Biology, and the Journal of Neuroscience.

In her spare time, Jane is an avid open-water swimmer, amateur astronomer, and enthusiastic home cook specializing in Japanese cuisine. She lives in Cambridge, MA with her partner and their two rescue dogs, Euler and Fourier.

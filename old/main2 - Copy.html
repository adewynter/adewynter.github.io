Extended Biography

Adrian de Wynter grew up in a small coastal town in Maine, where early encounters with tide pool ecosystems sparked a lifelong fascination with complex systems. She pursued her undergraduate studies at Stanford University, where she double-majored in Physics and Computer Science, graduating summa cum laude in 2005. It was during an undergraduate research project on neural signal processing that she first glimpsed the profound computational principles underlying biological brains.

She earned her PhD in Computational Neuroscience from University College London in 2010, working with Prof. Michael Hartley on recurrent network models of visual cortex. Her dissertation, "Temporal Dynamics of Predictive Coding in Hierarchical Neural Circuits," received the British Neuroscience Association's Outstanding Thesis Award and laid the groundwork for her current research program.

Following a postdoctoral fellowship at the Gatsby Computational Neuroscience Unit (2010–2013), Jane joined MIT as an Assistant Professor in 2013 and was promoted to Associate Professor with tenure in 2019. She founded the Neural Computation Lab, which currently comprises 4 postdocs, 8 PhD students, and 3 research engineers. The lab is known for its interdisciplinary approach, combining large-scale neural recordings in behaving animals with principled machine learning theory.

Beyond research, Jane is passionate about science communication and mentorship. She has mentored over 30 undergraduate researchers, many of whom have gone on to top PhD programs. She co-founded the Brains & Bytes public lecture series at MIT and serves on the editorial boards of Neuron, PLOS Computational Biology, and the Journal of Neuroscience.

In her spare time, Jane is an avid open-water swimmer, amateur astronomer, and enthusiastic home cook specializing in Japanese cuisine. She lives in Cambridge, MA with her partner and their two rescue dogs, Euler and Fourier.

Research Statement

Vision & Overview

The central question driving my research is deceptively simple: How does the brain compute? Despite decades of progress in neuroscience and artificial intelligence, we still lack a unified theoretical framework that explains how billions of neurons collectively give rise to perception, thought, and action. My lab addresses this gap by building computational models grounded in both neural data and machine learning theory.

We take a theory-driven experimental approach: we develop mathematical models that make precise, testable predictions about neural activity, then validate (or refute) those predictions using large-scale electrophysiology and two-photon calcium imaging in behaving animals. This tight loop between theory and experiment has allowed us to uncover principles that are invisible to purely data-driven approaches.

Predictive Coding & Hierarchical Inference

A major pillar of our work is the predictive coding framework, which posits that the brain continuously generates predictions about incoming sensory signals and updates its internal model based on prediction errors. We have developed deep predictive coding networks that not only match or exceed the performance of standard deep neural networks on vision benchmarks, but also quantitatively predict single-neuron responses in primary visual cortex (V1) and higher visual areas.

Our 2025 Science paper demonstrated that these models capture orientation tuning, surround suppression, and temporal adaptation dynamics—phenomena that conventional feedforward models fail to explain. We are now extending this framework to multi-modal integration, asking how the brain combines visual, auditory, and proprioceptive predictions into a coherent percept.

Decision-Making Under Uncertainty

How do neural circuits make rapid, reliable decisions from noisy sensory evidence? Our recent work in Nature Neuroscience identified specific recurrent circuit motifs in primate prefrontal and parietal cortex that implement near-optimal evidence accumulation. We showed that these motifs can be understood as implementing a form of amortized Bayesian inference—the brain learns to approximate the posterior distribution over task-relevant variables through recurrent dynamics, rather than computing it from scratch on each trial.

Brain-Inspired AI

A complementary line of research asks what AI can learn from neuroscience. Modern deep learning systems are extraordinarily capable but also extraordinarily wasteful—training a large language model can consume as much energy as five cars over their lifetimes. Biological brains, by contrast, operate on roughly 20 watts. We are developing neuromorphic learning algorithms that incorporate biological principles such as local learning rules, sparse coding, and event-driven computation to build AI systems that are orders of magnitude more energy-efficient.

Our NIH Pioneer Award (2025) funds a 5-year program to push this vision forward, with the goal of creating brain-inspired chips that can perform real-time sensory processing for medical devices such as next-generation cochlear implants and brain-computer interfaces.

Future Directions

Looking ahead, I see three frontiers that excite me most: (1) developing a computational theory of consciousness that can distinguish between models using empirical neural data; (2) building lifelong learning systems that, like biological brains, can continually acquire new skills without forgetting old ones; and (3) creating closed-loop neurotechnology that uses real-time neural decoding to restore lost sensory and motor function. Each of these challenges requires the kind of deep interdisciplinary collaboration that defines our lab's DNA.
